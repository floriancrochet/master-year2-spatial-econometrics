\section{Research question, hypotheses, and literature}

\subsection{Significance and motivation}

Despite the \emph{Solidarité et Renouvellement Urbain} (SRU) law mandating a minimum social housing share for larger municipalities, supply remains unevenly distributed across the territory. Whether this unevenness stems solely from local demographic pressures or also from interactions with neighbouring jurisdictions is an important policy question.

\medskip

\citet{chapelle2022} and \citet{maaoui2023} relate social housing supply to local social composition, while \citet{wang2025} and \citet{beenstock2015} document spatial dependence in housing provision across jurisdictions. Ignoring such interactions risks biased estimates and misleading policy conclusions.

\subsection{Research question}

\textbf{Research question.} \emph{To what extent is the density of social housing in a departement influenced by the social structure (solo-mother households, immigration) of that departement and its neighbours?}

\subsection{Hypotheses}

Three hypotheses guide the analysis:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{cl}
\toprule
\textbf{Hyp.} & \textbf{Statement} \\
\midrule
H1 & A departement's own vulnerability profile (solo-mother ratio, immigrant stock) is \\
   & positively associated with its social housing density. \\
H2 & The vulnerability profile of \emph{neighbouring} departements also affects local supply \\
   & (cross-border spillovers in social need). \\
H3 & Social housing density in a departement is positively associated with density \\
   & in its neighbours (inter-jurisdictional coordination or imitation). \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

H1 captures the local-need channel: territories with more vulnerable households should provide more social housing. H2 asks whether need in adjacent departements spills over --- for instance through inter-communal planning bodies or cross-border migration. H3 captures a direct outcome-to-outcome link, consistent with policy coordination or shared housing-market dynamics \citep{wang2025, beenstock2015}.

\subsection{Methodological approach}

To test these hypotheses we proceed as follows. A non-spatial OLS regression serves as the baseline; its residuals are checked for spatial autocorrelation (Moran's $I$, LM tests). If spatial dependence is detected, we estimate a series of spatial models --- SAR, SEM, SLX, and the general Spatial Durbin Model (SDM) --- under two neighbourhood definitions (Queen contiguity and $k$-nearest neighbours). The \citet{elhorst2010} general-to-specific procedure selects the most parsimonious specification not rejected by the data, and the \citet{lesage2009} impact decomposition separates own-departement effects from cross-departement spillovers. Full model definitions are presented in Section~4.

\section{Econometric methodology}

% Present the spatial econometric framework.

\subsection{Spatial weight matrices}
\label{sec:wmat}

The spatial weight matrix $W$ encodes which units are considered neighbours and how strongly. Four specifications are constructed, covering both contiguity and distance-based definitions, and compared in Table~\ref{tab:wmat_stats} below.

\subsubsection{Definitions}

\paragraph{Queen contiguity.}
Two departements are neighbours if their polygons share at least one boundary point (edge or corner). This symmetric matrix captures interactions through shared administrative borders.

\paragraph{$k$-nearest neighbours (KNN).}
Each departement's centroid is connected to its $k$ nearest centroids regardless of border adjacency. We consider $k \in \{1, 3, 6\}$: KNN-1 is a structural reference only (27 disconnected subgraphs); KNN-3 is the primary distance-based alternative; KNN-6 is a robustness check. KNN matrices are asymmetric by construction.

All matrices are \textbf{row-standardised} so that $Wy$ represents a weighted average of neighbours' values.

\subsection{Spatial econometric models}

Four nested models are considered. Their general form progresses from no spatial interaction (OLS) to the full Spatial Durbin Model (SDM).

\paragraph{OLS (benchmark).}
\begin{equation}
y_i = \mathbf{x}_i'\beta + \varepsilon_i, \quad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2 I)
\end{equation}
Spatial dependence in residuals from OLS signals model misspecification and is diagnosed via Lagrange multiplier tests.

\paragraph{SAR --- Spatial Autoregressive Model.}
\begin{equation}
y_i = \rho \sum_j w_{ij}\,y_j + \mathbf{x}_i'\beta + \varepsilon_i
\end{equation}
The scalar $\rho$ captures endogenous spatial lag: the social housing density of unit $i$ is partially determined by a weighted average of its neighbours' density (H3).

\paragraph{SEM --- Spatial Error Model.}
\begin{equation}
y_i = \mathbf{x}_i'\beta + u_i, \qquad u_i = \lambda \sum_j w_{ij}\,u_j + \varepsilon_i
\end{equation}
Spatial dependence confined to the error term ($\lambda$) indicates omitted spatially correlated confounders rather than true spillovers.

\paragraph{SLX --- Spatial Lag of X Model.}
\begin{equation}
y_i = \mathbf{x}_i'\beta + \sum_j w_{ij}\,\mathbf{x}_j'\theta + \varepsilon_i
\label{eq:slx}
\end{equation}
The SLX extends OLS by adding spatially weighted neighbours' covariates ($W\mathbf{X}$) while imposing $\rho = 0$. Direct and indirect effects equal $\hat{\beta}_k$ and $\hat{\theta}_k$ exactly --- no simulation is needed. The SLX is the $\rho = 0$ restriction of the SDM and can be tested against it via a likelihood ratio test.

\paragraph{SDM --- Spatial Durbin Model (preferred specification).}

The research question motivates the SDM as the primary model. It allows simultaneous testing of all three hypotheses within a single framework:

\begin{equation}
\underbrace{\mathrm{SHD}_{i,2024}}_{\text{Social Housing Density}} =
  \underbrace{\rho}_{\substack{\text{spatial auto-}\\\text{regressive coeff.}}}
  \underbrace{\sum_j w_{ij}\,\mathrm{SHD}_{j,2024}}_{W \cdot \mathrm{SHD}} +
  \underbrace{\mathbf{X}_{i,2022}'\beta}_{\text{local effects}} +
  \underbrace{\left(\sum_j w_{ij}\,\mathbf{X}_{j,2022}\right)'\theta}_{W\mathbf{X} \cdot \theta \;\text{(neighbour spillovers)}} +
  \varepsilon_i
\label{eq:sdm}
\end{equation}

where $\rho$ is the spatial autoregressive coefficient (H3), $W$ is the row-standardised $n \times n$ weight matrix, $\mathbf{X}_{i,2022}$ contains local covariates (SMR, $\ln$IMM, $\Delta$Pop, Pov) observed in 2022, $\beta$ captures local effects (H1), ${W\mathbf{X}}'{\theta}$ captures neighbour spillovers (H2), and $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$. The two-year lag between covariates (2022) and the dependent variable (2024) reduces simultaneity concerns.

\medskip
The SDM nests the SAR ($\theta = 0$), SLX ($\rho = 0$), and OLS ($\rho = \theta = 0$). Model selection follows \citet{elhorst2010}: estimate the SDM first, then test restrictions via likelihood ratio tests; retain the simplest non-rejected specification.

\paragraph{Interpretation via impact decomposition.}
Because $y$ appears on both sides of equation~\eqref{eq:sdm}, $\beta$ and $\theta$ do not directly equal marginal effects. Following \citet{lesage2009}, each regressor's total effect is decomposed into direct (own-departement) and indirect (cross-departement spillover) components via 1\,000 Monte Carlo draws.

\subsection{Elhorst (2010) model selection procedure}
\label{sec:elhorst}

Following \citet{elhorst2010}, the SDM is estimated first, then tested against restricted alternatives: SAR ($H_0\colon \theta = 0$), SEM ($H_0\colon \theta + \rho\beta = 0$), and SLX ($H_0\colon \rho = 0$). Tables~\ref{tab:sdm_compact},~\ref{tab:lr_tests}, and~\ref{tab:aic} report the estimates, LR tests, and AIC ranking.

\inputifexists{tables/sdm_compact.tex}

\inputifexists{tables/lr_tests_elhorst.tex}

\inputifexists{tables/aic_comparison.tex}

\paragraph{Restriction tests.}
All three LR tests reject their null (Table~\ref{tab:lr_tests}): SDM vs.\ SAR ($p < 0.01$), SDM vs.\ SEM ($p < 0.01$ Queen; $p = 0.01$ KNN-3), and SDM vs.\ SLX ($p < 0.001$). The \textbf{SDM is therefore retained}.

\paragraph{Weight matrix selection.}
The AIC ranking (Table~\ref{tab:aic}) selects \textbf{SDM Queen} as the best-fitting combination. This is consistent with the institutional logic: social housing administration follows administrative borders rather than straight-line distances.

\paragraph{SDM coefficient interpretation (Queen).}
Under the preferred specification (Table~\ref{tab:sdm_compact}), the spatial autoregressive parameter and the solo-mother ratio remain highly significant. The log-transformed immigrant stock, $\ln\mathrm{IMM}$, is also significant ($\hat{\beta} = 61.9$, $p < 0.05$), confirming that departements with larger immigrant populations provide more social housing, even after controlling for spatial structure. Its spatially lagged counterpart $W\!\times\!\ln\mathrm{IMM}$ is, however, not significant ($\hat{\theta} \approx 4.6$), indicating no exogenous cross-border spillover from neighbours' immigrant stock. Among the other spatially lagged covariates, $W\!\times\!\Delta\mathrm{Pop}$ is significant ($p < 0.05$). The impact decomposition below provides the proper marginal-effect reading.

\subsection{Model interpretation: SDM Queen impacts}
\label{sec:impacts}

Table~\ref{tab:impacts_combined} presents the impact decomposition for the preferred specification --- the SDM under Queen contiguity. Direct, indirect, and total effects are obtained via 1\,000 Monte Carlo draws from the asymptotic parameter distribution \citep{lesage2009}. In the SDM, the raw coefficients $\hat{\beta}$ and $\hat{\theta}$ do not directly equal marginal effects because spatial feedback loops redistribute every shock across the network; the impact decomposition accounts for this.

\inputifexists{tables/sdm_slx_impacts.tex}

\paragraph{Direct effects (H1).}
The solo-mother ratio has the largest direct effect, confirming \textbf{H1 for SMR}. The log-transformed immigrant stock also has a significant direct effect (\textbf{H1 for $\ln$IMM accepted}). Because $\ln\mathrm{IMM}$ enters the equation in log form, the direct effect of approximately~68 means that a 10\% increase in a departement's immigrant stock is associated with roughly~6.5 additional social housing units per 10\,000 inhabitants, holding all else constant. This confirms that, conditional on the other covariates and the spatial structure, the size of the local immigrant population is a significant predictor of departement-level social housing provision.

\paragraph{Indirect effects (H2).}
The indirect effect of the solo-mother ratio is negative, opposite to the hypothesised direction, suggesting concentration rather than diffusion of social housing within high-need clusters. \textbf{H2 for SMR is rejected}. The exogenous covariate spillover for log-immigrant stock ($W\!\times\!\ln\mathrm{IMM}$) is not significant in any specification ($\hat{\theta}_{\ln\mathrm{IMM}} \approx 4.6$, n.s.), so \textbf{H2 for $\ln$IMM is not confirmed}: there is no evidence that the immigrant population in neighbouring departements independently affects local social housing supply, beyond what is captured by the endogenous feedback channel ($\rho$).

\paragraph{Outcome-dependence channel (H3).}
\textbf{H3 is accepted}: the spatial autoregressive parameter $\hat{\rho}$ is highly significant (Queen, $p < 0.01$). Social housing density in a departement is significantly associated with supply levels in adjacent territories.

The SLX ($\rho = 0$ restriction of the SDM) was also estimated under both weight matrices; its LR test against the SDM is decisively rejected ($p < 0.001$), confirming that the outcome-to-outcome feedback channel ($\rho$) adds significant explanatory power beyond exogenous covariate spillovers alone. \section{Conclusion}

This paper investigated whether the density of social housing across French departements is shaped solely by local demographic pressures or also by interactions with neighbouring jurisdictions. Using a Spatial Durbin Model under Queen contiguity --- selected via the \citet{elhorst2010} general-to-specific procedure and confirmed by AIC --- we tested three hypotheses.

\textbf{H1} is supported: the solo-mother ratio is the strongest local predictor of social housing density, and the log-transformed immigrant stock is also significant ($p < 0.05$) across all spatial specifications. The log transformation corrects the heavy right-skew of the raw immigrant variable and yields a stable, positive association: departements with larger immigrant populations provide more social housing, consistent with a local-need channel.

\textbf{H2} is not confirmed in the hypothesised direction. The indirect effect of the solo-mother ratio is negative and only marginally significant, suggesting that social housing tends to concentrate within high-need clusters rather than spreading across neighbouring departements. The exogenous covariate spillover for log-immigrant stock ($W\!\times\!\ln\mathrm{IMM}$) is small and not significant, indicating no direct cross-border influence from neighbours' immigrant populations on local housing supply.

\textbf{H3} is accepted: the spatial autoregressive coefficient is highly significant ($p < 0.01$), indicating substantial interdependence in social housing provision between adjacent departements. This outcome-to-outcome link is the most robust spatial channel in the data and persists across weight matrix specifications.

The main policy implication is that social housing planning at the departement level cannot be treated in isolation. The significant spatial lag suggests that supply decisions in one jurisdiction are associated with --- and potentially influence --- supply in its neighbours. Coordinated regional approaches to social housing allocation are likely to be more effective than fragmented departement-by-departement planning.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the analysis is cross-sectional ($n = 94$): no causal claims can be made, and reverse causality --- social housing attracting vulnerable populations rather than responding to them --- cannot be ruled out. Second, the small sample constrains the degrees of freedom available for the SDM's eight regressors plus spatial lag, though Monte Carlo inference on impact measures partially addresses finite-sample concerns. Third, the two-year gap between covariates (2022) and the dependent variable (2024) reduces simultaneity but may introduce measurement error if social structure shifted over that period. Finally, the analysis does not account for unobserved political or historical determinants of housing stock, which likely vary across territories.
